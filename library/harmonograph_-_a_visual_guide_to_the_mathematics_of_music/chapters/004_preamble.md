#### sound made shape

In the mid-nineteenth century, Jules Lissajous, a French mathematician, devised an experiment: He found that if a small mirror was placed at the tip of a tuning fork, and a light beam was aimed at it, the vibration could be thrown onto a dark screen. When the tuning fork was struck, a small vertical line was produced, and if quickly cast sideways with another mirror it produced a sine wave (below).

Lissajous wondered what would happen if instead of casting the wave sideways he were to place another tuning fork at right angles to the first to give the lateral motion. He found that tuning forks with relative frequencies in simple ratios produced beautiful shapes, now known as Lissajous figures.

On the screen (opposite, top), we see the octave (2:1) as a figure eight, and below it various phases of the major and minor third. These were some of the first fleeting pictures of harmony, which were doubtless familiar to Professor Blackburn when he devised the harmonograph.
