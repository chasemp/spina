with thirds, sixths, and sevenths

By now it will be evident that each harmony displays its own distinct aesthetic character. Unison is simple and assertive. The octave introduces an emphatic flourish, and the fifth, while still fairly simple, has added elegance.

With the fourth the pattern becomes more complicated, though the design is still recognizable without counting the loops. The upper diagram opposite shows the fourth in open phase, the lower in closed phase. An increasing sophistication becomes apparent, and some of the closed phase and near-miss variants have a strange, exotic quality.

Introducing the perfect thirds of diatonic tuning increases the complexity. The major third (5:4) is found below the fourth, the interval between them, a *diatonic halftone*, working out as  $4:3 \div 5:4 = 16:15$ . A fourth and a major third (4:3 x 5:4) produce the major sixth, 5:3, a minor third (6:5) below the octave and a minor whole tone (10:9) above the fifth. Likewise, a fourth and a minor third (4:3 x 6:5) create the minor sixth (8:5), a major third (5:4) below the octave and a halftone (16:15) above the fifth.

A fifth and a major third  $(3:2\times5:4)$  produce the major seventh, 15:8, while a fifth and a minor third  $(3:2\times6:5)$  give the minor seventh, 9:5. These are the elements of the diatonic, or *just*, scale.
